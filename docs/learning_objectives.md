# Learning Objectives

Five objectives, one per framework pillar. Each describes what a UAP learner should be able to **do** after completing the training — not what they should know or believe, but what they can demonstrate in the puzzle interactions.

**Status:** drafted by Mike on 2026-04-22, reviewed and approved by John the same day for the prototype build. John explicitly flagged that the reviewer may adjust these during review. Treat as working targets, not final. Any change to an LO must trigger a corresponding update to the puzzle-to-competency matrix before the design brief is re-approved.

## The five pillars and their objectives

### Pillar 1 — Role boundaries

**LO1:** Distinguish between tasks a UAP may perform and tasks that must be left to the RN.

- **In scope for a UAP** (per OAR 851-047-0030, NASN, OSNA): comfort measures, observation, first aid within scope, documenting findings, tasks that have been both delegated by a specific RN AND ordered for a specific student.
- **Out of scope:** clinical assessment, diagnosis, independent nursing judgment, administration of any medication that is not both delegated and ordered.
- **Exercised in the playground scenario:** multiple decision points where the student, a teacher, or other staff ask the UAP to do something. Some are in scope (apply ice, help the student sit up, observe for changes). Some are out of scope (decide whether the wrist is broken, give Tylenol because a teacher suggested it).

### Pillar 2 — The Five Rights of Delegation

**LO2:** State the Five Rights of Delegation and recognize when any of the five are not met in a given situation.

- The Five Rights per NCSBN / ANA 2019 *National Guidelines for Nursing Delegation*: right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction / communication, right supervision / evaluation. Exact phrasing to be verified against the current NCSBN/ANA source — on the reviewer follow-up list.
- **Exercised in the playground scenario:** when the teacher asks the UAP to give Tylenol, the learner walks through the Five Rights and identifies which fail — right person (a teacher cannot delegate), right task (not delegated, ordered, or within scope absent an order), right direction (no RN direction given).

### Pillar 3 — Oregon scope of practice

**LO3:** Identify which common school-health actions are within Oregon's delegable scope for UAPs and which are not — including the emergency epinephrine / glucagon carve-out under ORS 433.800–433.830.

- **Delegable in Oregon:** general nursing care tasks that are stable and predictable, subcutaneous injectables, certain pre-measured IV additions, emergency epi / glucagon per ORS 433.800–433.830.
- **Not delegable:** the nursing process itself, IM injections (except emergency epi / glucagon), IV line initiation / discontinuation, anything requiring independent clinical judgment during performance.
- **Exercised in the playground scenario:** the scenario itself stays first-aid level, so Oregon-scope content appears as a short teaching moment plus a "is this in scope?" sort puzzle.

### Pillar 4 — Recognition and escalation

**LO4:** Given a changing student condition, identify red-flag signs that warrant RN notification, parent notification, or 911 — and sequence those notifications correctly.

- **Exercised in the playground scenario:** the student's condition evolves during the encounter. The UAP receives additional cues and must recognize thresholds at which the situation changes category, then sequence the calls: who first, who second, what to tell each.

### Pillar 5 — Documentation

**LO5:** Complete an incident record capturing what a supervising RN needs to follow up on: who, when, what was observed, what the UAP did, what the UAP reported, and to whom.

- **Exercised in the playground scenario:** at the end of the encounter the learner fills in an incident form. Good documentation (objective observations, actions taken, times, names) is distinguished from common failure modes (interpretation instead of observation, missing times, missing escalation chain).

## Mapping to puzzle-to-competency matrix

The full puzzle-to-competency matrix lives in the design brief (to be written in the design phase). Each LO must be exercised by at least one puzzle; no puzzle may exist without tying to at least one LO. The brief's ground rule: "If it doesn't map to a learning objective, cut it."
