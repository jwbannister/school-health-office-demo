# Oregon Law on RN Delegation to Unlicensed Assistive Personnel

Research pass completed 2026-04-22 by Mike. Primary reviewer target: the named supervising RN.

## Authority chain

- **Statute:** ORS Chapter 678 (Nurses) grants the Oregon State Board of Nursing rule-making authority over scope of practice and delegation.
- **Rules:** OAR Chapter 851 (OSBN). The key divisions:
  - Division 45 — Scope of practice standards for RNs and LPNs.
  - **Division 47 — Standards for Registered Nurse Delegation Process.** This is where the escape-room design decisions live.
- **2024 update:** As of 2024-09-01, OSBN adopted new rules pertaining to Division 47. Our references use the post-update text.

## OAR 851-047-0030 — the core delegation rule

### Step-by-step RN delegation process (section 3)

The RN must:

1. **Assess the client and determine stability.** Perform a nursing assessment and determine the client's condition is stable and predictable.
2. **Analyze the task.** Consider the nature of the task, its complexity, the risks involved, and the skills necessary.
3. **Determine safety.** Assess whether an unlicensed person can perform the task safely without direct RN supervision.
4. **Set reassessment frequency.** Establish how often the client's condition needs reassessment.
5. **Evaluate the caregiver.** "Evaluate the skills, ability and willingness of the unlicensed persons."
6. **Teach.** Provide instruction covering the proper procedure, necessity, risks, side effects, appropriate responses, client observation, and documentation methods.
7. **Observe.** "Observe the unlicensed persons performing the task to ensure that they perform the task safely and accurately."
8. **Leave written guidance.** Step-by-step procedural instructions with signs/symptoms and response protocols.
9. **Enforce client-specificity.** "Instruct the unlicensed persons that the task being taught and delegated is specific to this client only."

### What CAN be delegated in Oregon

- General nursing care tasks that are stable, predictable, and do not require independent clinical judgment during performance.
- Subcutaneous injectable medications (section 1).
- Certain IV medication administration under strict conditions (sections 8–9): flushing lines, adding pre-measured medications, changing fluid bags only.
- **Emergency epinephrine and glucagon per ORS 433.800–433.830** (statutory carve-out).

### What CANNOT be delegated

- "The nursing process in its entirety" (section 1(a)) — assessment, judgment, reassessment stay with the RN.
- Intramuscular medications (section 11), except emergency epinephrine / glucagon per ORS 433.800–433.830.
- IV line initiation or discontinuation (section 8(e)).
- IV medications beyond those specified in sections 8–9.
- Anything requiring interpretation or independent decision making during performance.

### Ongoing supervision requirements (section 4)

- **Initial supervisory visit** must occur within at least 60 days from the initial date of delegation (section 4(d)).
- **Periodic reassessment** must verify the client remains stable and predictable, and that the caregiver remains capable and willing. May be on-site or via technology that lets the RN visualize both the client and the caregiver.
- **Interval frequency factors** (section 4(f)): task complexity, prior teaching experience, RN relationship length, client stability, caregiver skill demonstration, caregiver's ability to recognize changes, and availability of backup healthcare professionals.
- **Maximum interval** between supervisory visits is no greater than every 180 days (section 4(g)).

### Documentation (section 3(k))

The RN must document:

- The nursing assessment and client condition.
- Rationale for safe delegation.
- Caregiver skills, ability, and willingness.
- Task competency confirmation.
- Written instructions including risks, side effects, and reporting protocols.
- Evidence that instruction was client-specific and non-transferable.
- Reassessment frequency with rationale based on client needs.
- Supervision / re-evaluation frequency with caregiver competency rationale.
- RN accountability statement that "the Registered Nurse takes responsibility for delegating the task to the unlicensed persons, and ensures that supervision will occur."

**Governing principle** (section 1(b)): "The responsibility, accountability and authority for teaching and delegation of tasks of nursing care to unlicensed persons shall remain with the Registered Nurse."

## Implications for training design

1. **Our escape room cannot legally substitute for RN-led teach-back.** The rule places the teach + observe + validate sequence on the RN personally and makes it client-specific. Any training product that claimed to certify a UAP to perform a delegated task on unspecified students would misrepresent Oregon law.
2. **Foundational content is the right fit.** Before the RN's client-specific teach-back, UAPs need to understand their role, the Five Rights framework, Oregon scope of practice, escalation, recognition of deterioration, and documentation. Those are transferable and can be taught via web-based training.
3. **Scope boundaries are teachable content.** UAPs should leave the training knowing what they cannot do (IM injections outside emergency epi/glucagon, IV manipulation, anything requiring judgment during performance) and what to say when asked to do something out of scope.
4. **Emergency epi/glucagon is a special case.** These are statutorily carved out; training should treat them as a specific protocol with specific recognition criteria, not lump them with routine medication administration.
5. **Documentation matters.** UAPs who document well make the RN's supervisory job possible. Training should model good documentation.

## Oregon context

- Per the OHA 2023 Oregon School Nurse Survey and ODE legislative reports, ~86% of Oregon school nurses used care delegation during the 2022–23 school year.
- On average, an Oregon school RN trains roughly 9 UAPs to perform delegated nursing care.
- Commonly delegated tasks in Oregon schools include medication administration, tube feeding, urinary catheterization, ostomy or ileostomy care, tracheostomy care, specialized asthma/respiratory care, and cardiac pacemaker monitoring.
