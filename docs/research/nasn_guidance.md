# NASN — Current Guidance on Delegation in Schools

Research pass 2026-04-22.

## Current status of NASN's delegation position

- The prior NASN Position Statement "Nursing Delegation in the School Setting" was **retired January 2022.**
- Current guidance lives in two places:
  1. The NASN Health & Practice delegation topic page.
  2. **S081 — *Principles for Practice: Nursing Delegation to Unlicensed Assistive Personnel in the School Setting, 2nd Edition*.** This is the authoritative NASN resource now. Not retrieved in full during this pass; on the reviewer follow-up list for verification.

## Core NASN definitions and positions

- Delegation is: "the assignment by the school nurse – not a school administrator – to a competent unlicensed individual (also called unlicensed assistive personnel [UAP]) the performance of a selected nursing task in a selected situation for an individual student."
- Only the RN can delegate. Principals, doctors, and parents **cannot** delegate nursing tasks.
- "The nursing process can never be delegated" — assessment, nursing judgment, and evaluation of outcomes stay with the RN.
- The school nurse is responsible for:
  - Facilitating UAP training.
  - Evaluating UAP competence.
  - Providing ongoing supervision.
  - Evaluating student health outcomes.

## NASN tools and resources

- **Recommended Qualifications for the Unlicensed Assistive Personnel** (NASN PDF). Baseline qualifications for who is appropriate as a UAP.
- **Sample Skills Checklist for Unlicensed Assistive Personnel Training** (NASN PDF). A template competency-validation checklist — useful as a model for how our training's output should look to an RN.

## Deference to state law

NASN explicitly directs state-specific questions to the state Board of Nursing. For this project that means **Oregon's OAR Chapter 851 Division 47 governs, not NASN.** Where NASN and Oregon differ, Oregon wins.

## The Five Rights of Delegation

The Five Rights framework (right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction/communication, right supervision/evaluation) is the **NCSBN/ANA** framework, not originally NASN's. The NASN guidance and the OSNA toolkit both reference it, and the current authoritative text lives in the NCSBN/ANA *National Guidelines for Nursing Delegation* (2019).

This is important for our training: we will teach the Five Rights as the lens through which a UAP understands the RN's role and their own responsibilities. The exact phrasing we teach must match the current 2019 NCSBN/ANA wording; that is an open item for the RN reviewer to verify.

## Implications for training design

1. The NASN Sample Skills Checklist is a reasonable structural model for the competency evidence our training generates. If we produce a completion record that maps to the skills-checklist format, an RN can use it as the starting point for the client-specific teach-back they are required to conduct.
2. "The nursing process can never be delegated" is a teachable concept. UAPs should leave training knowing that assessment, interpretation, and judgment are the RN's jobs, not theirs — and knowing when their observations need to be escalated to the RN rather than acted on.
3. NASN's alignment with state Boards of Nursing reinforces that anything we do must survive Oregon-specific scrutiny by the named RN reviewer.
