# OSNA — Oregon School Nurses Association Delegation Toolkit

Research pass 2026-04-22.

## OSNA's definition and legal framing

OSNA defines nursing delegation as the transfer of responsibility for a nursing procedure from the RN to an unregulated assistive person (UAP), with the RN retaining accountability for outcomes. OSNA grounds the process in:

- The Oregon Nurse Practice Act (OAR Chapter 851).
- **Chapter 851, Division 47** — Standards for Registered Nurse Delegation Process.

This is the same authority we cited in `oregon_delegation_law.md` — OSNA's toolkit is the practitioner-facing operationalization of the state rule.

## Six-step OSNA delegation process

OSNA provides documentation templates covering six phases:

1. **Assessment & Determination** — evaluate student needs and circumstances to determine whether delegation is appropriate.
2. **Guidance & Direction** — nurse delivers evidence-based instruction on the procedure.
3. **Validation** — nurse assesses UAP competency after training.
4. **Authorization** — RN documents the initial delegation agreement.
5. **Periodic Inspection & Evaluation** — ongoing monitoring and reassessment.
6. **Ending Delegation** — formal termination when the student no longer needs the delegated care, when the UAP is no longer appropriate, or when the nurse withdraws the delegation.

This six-step framing maps cleanly onto the OAR 851-047-0030 language. Our training's learning objectives should reflect that the UAP is a participant in steps 2–5 (guidance, validation, ongoing evaluation) and that steps 1, 4, and 6 are the RN's calls.

## Training and competency requirements

OSNA's toolkit emphasizes: "the school nurse completes the assessment of the student, the UAP training, the evaluation and validation of UAP competence, and provides ongoing supervision."

Templates are designed to accommodate both single-UAP/single-procedure situations and more complex multi-UAP/multi-procedure situations.

## Key decision factors OSNA highlights

- Student assessment and supporting dynamics.
- Nursing competence.
- Degree of supervision available.
- Procedure complexity.
- UAP competency level.

## Implications for training design

1. **Our training fits squarely into OSNA's step 2 (Guidance & Direction).** It is a pre-validation, foundational-knowledge component the RN can build on during the client-specific teach-back.
2. **Output compatibility.** If the training's completion record can slot into the OSNA documentation templates the RN is already using for step 4 (Authorization), we reduce friction. Worth aligning our CSV/JSON export schema with OSNA's template fields during design.
3. **Ending delegation is a teachable concept.** UAPs should know that delegation is not permanent — it can and should be withdrawn when circumstances change, and recognizing those circumstances (student no longer stable, UAP no longer willing/able) is part of their role.
