# Scenario Outline — Playground Tumble

The narrative spine of the prototype. Each beat is a framework pillar exercised through a specific moment in the scenario. Draft for John's approval; clinical specifics (phrasing, thresholds, documentation examples) carry over to the reviewer's review list.

## Setting and cast

- **Setting:** a school health office on a regular school day, mid-morning. Clean, warm, a desk and a chair and a short exam-style cot, cabinets labeled, a visible incident-report form on the desk. No simulated emergency lighting or dramatic cues.
- **The learner:** "you are the school health assistant today." No name, so the role is foregrounded.
- **The student:** Mia, a 4th-grader (age 9). Friendly, chatty, a little shaken. No delegated-care plan on file — she is a routine visit, not a student on whom any specific task has been delegated.
- **The teacher:** Mr. Delgado. Reasonable under pressure, but he has a class waiting and would like to hand this off quickly.

## Opening frame

Mr. Delgado walks Mia into the office. She's holding her left wrist and she's a little pink in the cheeks.

Mr. Delgado: "She took a tumble on the monkey bars at recess. Can you give her some Tylenol and send her back? I've got a class waiting."

Mia: "I'm okay. My wrist kinda hurts and I bumped my head a little but I'm fine."

**That opening sets up all five pillars naturally.** The rest of the scenario lets the learner work through them.

## Beat 1 — Role boundaries (LO1)

### What the learner is doing

Parsing the opening: a teacher made a request, a student described an injury, the learner has to decide what their first action is before doing anything else.

### Puzzle mechanic

Multiple choice, one correct answer, feedback on all four.

### The choices

1. Go to the cabinet and get Tylenol, as Mr. Delgado asked.
2. Tell Mr. Delgado "no, I can't do that" and send Mia back to class with him.
3. Ask Mia to sit down, ask her what happened and how she feels, and let Mr. Delgado know you'll take it from here.
4. Call Mia's parent immediately to pick her up.

**Correct:** option 3.

### Why

Your first job as a UAP is not to decide, not to dispense, and not to dismiss. It is to receive the student, observe calmly, and gather the facts a nurse would want. Option 1 crosses a scope line; option 2 hands the problem back to the teacher without gathering information; option 4 jumps past the current situation to an escalation you haven't earned yet. Option 3 buys you the time and information you need to do everything else well.

### Feedback language for each option

- Option 1: "Not yet. Giving medication is a separate decision — we'll talk through it in a moment. The very first move is to find out what actually happened."
- Option 2: "Not this one. Mia is in your office because something happened. Whether she stays or goes back is a decision for later, after you know more."
- Option 3: "Yes. You're not diagnosing, you're not deciding yet — you're gathering facts a nurse would want. That's squarely within what a UAP does."
- Option 4: "Too fast. Calling a parent is often the right move, but not before you understand what you're calling about."

## Beat 2 — The Five Rights of Delegation (LO2)

### What the learner is doing

Mr. Delgado is still standing there: "Just give her the Tylenol so I can get back." The learner has to either refuse or escalate the Tylenol request and — more importantly — understand why.

### Puzzle mechanic

Match each of the Five Rights to "why this request fails that Right." Drag-and-drop or click-to-match.

### The Five Rights and what fails for this request

- **Right task** — Is giving Tylenol a task that has been delegated for Mia? **No.** There is no RN delegation for Tylenol on file for Mia.
- **Right circumstance** — Is this a stable, predictable, delegated scenario? **No.** It's an unplanned injury; any medication decision requires an order.
- **Right person** — Who is asking, and can they delegate? **No.** A teacher cannot delegate nursing tasks; only an RN can.
- **Right direction / communication** — Has an RN given instruction to do this? **No.** There is no communication from the nurse about this.
- **Right supervision / evaluation** — Is the RN supervising this action and available to evaluate? **No.** The RN is not in this conversation.

### Outcome and script

Provide the learner with a short polite refusal script they can repeat in real life:

> "Mr. Delgado, I can't give any medication without an order from our nurse. Let me take care of Mia here — she's in good hands. You can head back to your class."

### Why

The Five Rights is a reusable checklist. Any request a UAP gets — from a teacher, a parent, a student, another staff member — can be run through it. For this request, every one of the five fails. That is the point.

## Beat 3 — Oregon scope of practice (LO3)

### What the learner is doing

Mr. Delgado leaves. Now it's the learner and Mia. Before doing anything else, the learner needs a quick read on which actions are in or out of their scope.

### Puzzle mechanic

Sort a set of possible actions into three buckets: **In scope for UAP**, **Out of scope**, **Escalate to RN**.

### The items and correct buckets

| Action | Bucket |
|---|---|
| Apply an ice pack to Mia's wrist | In scope |
| Help Mia sit down and offer her water | In scope |
| Decide whether Mia's wrist is broken | Out of scope (clinical assessment / diagnosis) |
| Fill in your observations on the incident form | In scope |
| Give Mia ibuprofen she has in her backpack | Out of scope (medication administration absent order) |
| Call Mia's parent to notify them | In scope (per school protocol) |
| Call the school nurse to describe what you're seeing | Escalate to RN (and always a safe move) |
| Clear Mia to go back to class because she "seems fine" | Out of scope (implies assessment you can't do) |
| Start an IV | Out of scope (statutorily not delegable in Oregon) |
| Administer emergency epinephrine if Mia has a known allergy action plan on file | In scope (per ORS 433.800–433.830) — teachable exception |

### Why

UAPs can do a lot; UAPs cannot do some specific things; in Oregon the boundaries are drawn by OAR 851-047-0030 with a statutory carve-out for emergency epi / glucagon. The puzzle teaches both the boundaries and the carve-out so a learner doesn't over-generalize "UAPs can't give medication" and miss the one case where they can.

## Beat 4 — Recognition and escalation (LO4)

### What the learner is doing

It's been 15 minutes. Mia has an ice pack on her wrist and has been sitting quietly. The learner notices something has changed.

### The cue (prototype version)

Mia says: "I feel kind of weird. Can I lie down? My head hurts more than before and I'm really sleepy."

### Why this cue

This is a canonical concussion red flag — increasing headache, altered mentation, sudden somnolence after a head bump. It cleanly triggers escalation logic without requiring deep clinical knowledge from the learner. (Alternative cues — worsening wrist swelling, cold fingers, new vomiting — stay on the bench for later variants.)

### Puzzle mechanic

Two steps.

**Step A — Sequence the calls.** The learner places three calls in the correct order: RN, parent, 911. For a change-of-condition like this, the right sequence is:

1. **School RN first.** The RN needs to know immediately so they can decide whether to come in, advise, or direct a 911 call. RN notification is both a clinical and a supervisory obligation.
2. **Parent.** Parents need to be told promptly and in plain terms, and the RN may want to direct how that conversation goes.
3. **911.** Called if the RN advises it, if the RN is not reachable and the change warrants it, or if the condition worsens rapidly (unresponsive, seizure, vomiting with altered mentation).

**Step B — Draft the scripts.** The learner picks the correct short script for each call from a pool of correct and incorrect options.

Correct examples:

- To RN: "Hi, this is [UAP name] at [school]. Mia [last name], 4th grade, came in 15 minutes ago after a playground fall. She bumped her head and hurt her wrist. She's now saying her head hurts more, she feels weird, and she wants to lie down. She's awake but drowsy. Can you come, or what would you like me to do?"
- To parent: "Hi, this is [UAP name] from [school]. Mia had a fall at recess. She's here in the office. She bumped her head and her wrist. Our nurse is aware. Mia's awake but not quite herself — we'd like you to come pick her up as soon as you can. If anything changes we'll call 911."

Incorrect examples (in the pool for contrast):

- To RN: "Hey, Mia's not feeling great. Should I send her home?" (vague, missing facts, no time, no specifics).
- To parent: "Mia probably has a concussion but we're not sure. Come get her." (interpretive / diagnostic; outside UAP scope).

### Why

Escalation is about sequence and content, not just urgency. A UAP who calls in the wrong order or with the wrong content makes the RN's job harder and may put the student at risk.

## Beat 5 — Documentation (LO5)

### What the learner is doing

Mia's parent is on their way. The RN has been briefed. Before the encounter ends the learner fills in the incident form.

### Puzzle mechanic

A blank incident form with labeled fields. A pool of candidate entries mixed correct and incorrect. The learner drags or selects the right entry for each field.

### Fields and example entries

**Time of incident**
- Correct: "10:12 AM."
- Incorrect: "Mid-morning."

**What the student reported**
- Correct: "Mia said she fell off the monkey bars, hurt her left wrist, and bumped her head."
- Incorrect: "Mia fell and is probably fine."

**Observations**
- Correct: "On arrival Mia was alert and oriented, holding her left wrist, reported mild headache. At 10:27 AM she reported an increased headache and drowsiness."
- Incorrect: "Mia seemed okay at first then went downhill." (interpretive, no times, no specifics)

**Actions taken**
- Correct: "Applied ice pack to left wrist at 10:15 AM. Observed for 15 minutes. Notified school nurse [name] at 10:28 AM per change in condition. Notified parent [name] at 10:31 AM and arranged pickup."
- Incorrect: "Helped her out and called people." (vague)

**Escalations**
- Correct: "School nurse [name] notified at 10:28 AM. Parent [name] notified at 10:31 AM. 911 not called — RN advised parent pickup."
- Incorrect: "Called for help." (no names, no times, no decisions)

### Why

Good UAP documentation is objective, timestamped, named, and complete. An RN reviewing the record should be able to reconstruct what happened without needing to ask follow-up questions. Bad documentation wastes the RN's time and, if the situation becomes a complaint or a legal matter, cannot survive scrutiny.
